Investor Guides

Practical guides for buying property at auction

First-Time Auction Buyer Checklist

  1. Research the auction house - understand their terms, buyer's premium, and format
  2. Register to bid - most houses require advance registration with ID
  3. Get finance pre-approved - secure a bridging loan decision in principle before auction day
  4. Instruct a solicitor - have them review the legal pack before you bid
  5. Arrange a survey - view the property and assess its condition
  6. Set your maximum bid - include ALL costs: stamp duty, legal fees, buyer's premium, refurb
  7. Prepare your deposit - typically 10% of the hammer price, payable on the day
  8. Attend as observer first - watch an auction before bidding to learn the rhythm
  9. Bid decisively - confident, clear bids show you're serious
  10. Have your solicitor on standby - exchange happens immediately after the hammer falls

How to Read a Legal Pack

The legal pack is the most important document in any auction purchase. Key items to check:

  • Title deeds - confirm ownership, boundaries, and any rights of way
  • Special conditions of sale - look for unusual completion timelines, additional fees, or restrictions
  • Local authority searches - check for planning issues, conservation areas, or proposed developments
  • Environmental searches - flood risk, contaminated land, subsidence
  • Leasehold information - if leasehold, check remaining term, ground rent, service charges
  • Seller's property information form - disputes, alterations, guarantees

Top tip: Always have a solicitor review the legal pack. The cost is small compared to the risk of missing something critical.

Bridging Finance Explained

Bridging loans are short-term finance designed for auction purchases and property development.

  • Typical rates - from 0.44% per month, depending on LTV and property type
  • LTV - up to 75% of the property value (some lenders up to 80%)
  • Term - usually 3-18 months
  • Speed - funds can be released in as little as 7 days
  • Arrangement fee - typically 1-2% of the loan amount
  • Exit strategy - you'll need a clear plan: refinance to mortgage, or sell

Having a bridging loan pre-approved before auction day means you can bid with confidence, knowing you can complete within the required 28-day window.

Assessing Property Condition

What to look for when viewing an auction lot:

  • Structural issues - cracks in walls, sagging roofline, damp patches, bowing walls
  • Roof condition - missing tiles, lead flashing condition, gutter state
  • Electrics & plumbing - age of systems, visible wiring condition, boiler type and age
  • Damp & timber - rising damp, penetrating damp, woodworm, dry rot
  • Windows & doors - single/double glazed, frame condition, draughts
  • External areas - boundary walls, drainage, access, Japanese knotweed

Budget rule of thumb: For heavy refurb projects, budget £1,000-1,500/sqm. For light refurb, £300-600/sqm.

Understanding Auction Fees

The hammer price is just the starting point. Here are the fees to budget for:

  • Buyer's premium - 0% to 4.2% (inc. VAT) depending on the auction house
  • Admin/reservation fee - some online auctions charge £5,000-6,000+ for conditional sales
  • Stamp Duty (SDLT) - standard rates apply, plus 5% surcharge for additional properties
  • Legal fees - solicitor costs £1,000-2,500 for a standard auction purchase
  • Survey - £300-800 depending on type and property size
  • Bridging finance costs - arrangement fee (1-2%) plus monthly interest

Example: A £150,000 hammer price lot could cost £170,000-180,000 all-in once you include all fees and taxes.

Post-Auction: What Happens Next

The 28-day countdown to completion starts the moment the hammer falls:

  • Day 0 (Auction day) - pay 10% deposit, exchange contracts, sign memorandum of sale
  • Days 1-3 - instruct solicitor to proceed, confirm bridging loan application
  • Days 3-10 - lender valuation arranged and completed
  • Days 10-20 - lender issues formal offer, solicitor completes searches
  • Days 20-25 - final checks, funds drawn down from lender
  • Day 28 - completion deadline. The remaining 90% is paid, keys are released

Critical: If you fail to complete, you lose your deposit AND may be liable for the seller's losses. Always have finance confirmed before bidding.

Auction Investor Tips

Key insights for making smart auction purchases

Always View the Legal Pack

Before bidding, review the legal pack thoroughly. It contains the title deeds, special conditions, searches, and any issues. Instruct a solicitor before the auction, not after.

Know Your Maximum Bid

Calculate your maximum bid including all costs: buyer's premium, stamp duty, legal fees, refurbishment costs, and finance costs. Never exceed your number in the heat of bidding.

Understand the Area

Research comparable sales, rental yields, and local demand before bidding. A good price means nothing if the area doesn't support your exit strategy.

Have Finance Ready

When the hammer falls, you're legally committed. Having a bridging loan pre-approved means you can bid knowing you can complete. Don't risk losing your deposit.

Watch Buyer's Premium

Some auction houses charge 0%, others up to 4% + VAT on top of the hammer price. This can add thousands to your costs. Always factor this into your maximum bid.

Attend Before You Bid

Visit a few auctions as an observer before committing money. Understand the pace, the atmosphere, and how experienced bidders operate. Online auctions also have preview periods.
